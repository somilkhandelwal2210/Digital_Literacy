Online Safety Tips for College Students

1. Money arrives without any code needed - never type in your UPI PIN just to get funds. Only when moving cash out or peeking at your total does that number matter.
2. A quick glance before hitting send can save trouble later. That UPI ID needs a second look - make sure it is exactly who you think you are paying. Watch the name pop up on your display; does it feel right? Stare at the screen until you are certain. Tiny errors might snowball into serious issues eventually. A quick double-check now prevents headaches afterward. That moment you spend verifying? Worth it when chaos looms.
3. Besides long passwords, mix up each one across your college login, email, mailbox, and online profiles. Wherever possible - especially on key services - turn on two-step checks that add extra layers beyond just a password. Activate Two-Step Login (2SA) everywhere it's offered.
4. Sometimes messages pop up saying you won something big - ignore those if they arrive out of nowhere. A sudden notice about your bank being frozen might feel urgent, yet it could be false. Messages promising free streaming services often hide risky shortcuts. Links sent through text, email, or chat apps can lead somewhere dangerous when they seem too good to be true. Win alerts from unknown sources usually aren’t what they claim. An unexpected alert about money problems may push you to act fast - pause before tapping anything. Free access offers arriving by message rarely come with honest intent.
5. When handling money matters like tuition payments, skip the campus hotspot - opt for a secure connection instead.
6. Got a code sent to your phone? Keep it to yourself. Even someone saying they’re from the bank shouldn’t get it. Your CVV - those three digits on the back - isn’t for sharing either. Expiry date of your card? That stays private too. It doesn’t matter who asks - even if they sound official. Trust only what you see in front of you, not voices on calls.
7. When it comes to social media, lock down your profile so only close connections can see what you post. Anyone asking to connect should be someone you’ve met face to face. Seeing a request from a stranger? Best left unaccepted. Your circle stays small by design - no need to grow it beyond real-world ties. Privacy starts with who gets let in
8. Careful about who made an app before you install it. Look up what others have said instead of just trusting the rating. Pay attention to what access it asks for on your device. Getting software from random sites can slip risks onto your phone.
